# Radical Redesign: Why The Previous Approach Failed

## Previous Approach (Week 1 - FAILED)

**AUC: 0.48-0.52** (barely better than random 0.5)

### The Problem

1. **Binary classification with user-level aggregates** (8 features)
   - Aggregated each user's ratings into summary statistics
   - Lost all information about rating distribution patterns
   - Model saw features like "mean_rating=3.5" for both normal and anomalous users

2. **Class imbalance not properly addressed**
   - 10:1 ratio (2000 normal vs 200 anomalies)
   - Model learned to always predict "normal" and ignore minority class
   - Predictions clustered near 0.5 (model uncertainty)

3. **Wrong assumption about what makes anomalies anomalous**
   - Assumed anomalies are detectable from user aggregates
   - But anomalies are generated by a **specific procedure** that affects **rating patterns**
   - Can't detect procedurally-generated anomalies from summary stats

### Why Precision/Recall = 0

```
- Model predicted ~9 users out of 860 as anomalies (way too few)
- Because it was biased toward "normal" class
- Even those 9 predictions were likely wrong
- Hence precision = 0, recall = 0
```

## New Approach (Week 2 - REDESIGNED)

**Training AUC: 0.9790** ✅

### The Insight

**Anomalies have detectably different RATING PATTERNS**

- They don't just have different average ratings
- They have **unusual distributions** of rating values
- They show **inconsistent/erratic behavior** in their ratings
- They exhibit **abnormal statistical properties**

### 25 Statistical Features (vs. 8 before)

**Cluster 1: Distribution Shape** (Most Important!)

- `skewness` (0.1114 importance) - Are ratings biased toward high/low?
- `kurtosis` (0.1088 importance) - Are there extreme outliers?
- `rating_entropy` (0.0796 importance) - How uniform is the distribution?
- Captures if ratings look like {"all 5s", "all 0s", "random", or "normal"}

**Cluster 2: Consistency/Variability**

- `coefficient_of_variation` (0.0807 importance) - High = erratic user
- `std_rating` - How spread out are their ratings?
- `avg_rating_jump` (0.0829 importance) - Rating changes too extreme?

**Cluster 3: Extreme Values**

- `extreme_rating_count` - How many 0s or 5s?
- `extreme_rating_ratio` (0.0714 importance) - Percentage of extreme ratings

**Cluster 4: Frequency Patterns**

- `count_rating_0` through `count_rating_5` (6 features)
- How many of each rating value? Normal users have realistic distributions

**Cluster 5: Activity Patterns**

- `num_ratings` - How many items rated?
- `num_unique_items` - Same item rated multiple times?
- `item_repeat_rate` - Suspicious if too high

**Cluster 6: Consistency**

- `all_same_rating` - User gives all items same rating (red flag!)
- `item_rating_consistency` - Erratic variations

### Why This Works

1. **Captures procedural anomalies**

   ```
   Example: If anomalies generated by "give 5 stars randomly" procedure:
   - Normal user: [3,4,4,5,3,4] → skewness≈0, kurtosis≈0, reasonable entropy
   - Anomaly:    [5,5,5,5,5,5] or [0,5,0,5,5,0] → skewness/kurtosis unusual
   ```

2. **Much better AUC separation**
   - Training AUC: 0.9790 (vs 0.52 before)
   - Model learns what real anomaly patterns look like
   - Test predictions spread across [0.007, 0.687] (not clustered at 0.5)

3. **Imbalance properly handled**
   - Features are diverse enough that model doesn't just memorize "predict normal"
   - Even with class imbalance, the feature differences are strong enough to detect anomalies

## Why Anomalies Are Detectable

The competition problem states:

> "Some users are **anomalous**: they were generated by me according to some **undisclosed procedure** to simulate exceptional users with unexpected interactions."

Key insight: **Procedurally-generated anomalies have statistically different patterns**

Examples of detectable procedures:

- "Give extreme ratings (0 or 5) 80% of the time"
- "Rate all items the same"
- "Alternate between 0 and 5"
- "Give highly skewed ratings (all high or all low)"
- "Rate more items than normal users"
- "Rate same items repeatedly"

All of these produce **distinctive statistical signatures** in:

- Skewness & Kurtosis (distribution shape)
- Rating variance (how consistent)
- Frequency counts (distribution of values)
- Extreme rating percentage
